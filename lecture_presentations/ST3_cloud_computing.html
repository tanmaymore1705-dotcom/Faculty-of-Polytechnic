>> The hook

"There is no cloud.
It's just someone else's computer."

The word "cloud" hides a very ordinary truth. When your photos are "in the cloud", they're on a hard drive, in a building, in a city somewhere, owned by a company like Amazon or Google.

The genius isn't the technology — it's the business model: rent computing by the minute, so you never have to buy, house, or babysit a server yourself.

Reframe for today: "the cloud" = a giant pile of the exact computers from ST1, in a building, connected by the internet from ST2, that you rent instead of own.
>> 02 / 06 · The problem it solves

Why owning your own servers hurts

OLD WAY · BUY
  • Spend lakhs upfront before serving one user.
  • Guess your peak load — guess wrong, you crash or overpay.
  • Wait weeks for new hardware to ship.
  • Power, cooling, security, repairs — all yours.
CLOUD WAY · RENT
  • Pay pennies per hour, only while you use it.
  • Need 100 more servers for one busy night? Click. Done in seconds.
  • Turn them off in the morning, stop paying.
  • Power, cooling, repairs — their problem.
The unlock: a two-person startup can now use the same world-class infrastructure as a giant bank — and pay only for what they touch.
>> 03 / 06 · Inside a data center

The "cloud" is this building

A warehouse the size of a stadium, filled with rack after rack of the computers from ST1 — tens of thousands of them, running 24/7.

Racks of servers

Stripped-down computers — just CPU, RAM, storage, network. No screen, stacked in metal shelves.

Power & cooling

Backup generators, batteries, and massive A/C — heat is the enemy. Cooling can cost as much as the computing.

Redundancy

Two of everything. A drive dies, a server fails — your data is copied elsewhere, you never notice.

Why near rivers and cold places? Data centers drink electricity and need cooling. Providers build them where power is cheap and the climate helps — Iceland, Oregon, northern India.
>> 04 / 06 · The three service models

Three ways to rent — how much do they manage?

IaaS
Infrastructure

You rent a bare computer. You install everything. Most control, most work.

e.g. AWS EC2

PaaS
Platform

You just upload your code. They run it. No server admin. The sweet spot for beginners.

e.g. Heroku, Render

SaaS
Software

You just use the app in a browser. Someone else built and runs all of it.

e.g. Gmail, Canva

The pizza analogy. IaaS = they give you the kitchen, you cook. PaaS = they cook, you bring the recipe. SaaS = they serve you the pizza. Same dinner, different amount of work.
>> 05 / 06 · The trick that makes it cheap

One real computer, many "virtual" ones

A single powerful server is sliced by software into many isolated machines. You rent a slice; your neighbour rents another; neither of you can see the other.

Containers (like Docker) take this further — lighter, faster slices that start in seconds. Same idea: pack many tenants safely onto one machine, so it's never sitting idle.
>> The superpower

Scaling — grow in seconds, shrink at midnight

VERTICAL · bigger

Give the one server more CPU and RAM. Simple, but there's a ceiling — and you must reboot.

HORIZONTAL · more

Add more servers and split the traffic between them. Nearly unlimited — this is how Netflix survives a new release.

Elasticity is the magic word: scale up automatically when traffic spikes, scale back down when it's quiet — so on Diwali night you run 50 servers, and at 4 a.m. you run 2 and pay for 2. You pay for the shape of your day.
>> 06 / 06 · The meter is running

You pay like electricity, not like a car

No big purchase. A meter ticks for compute time, storage, and data sent out — billed to the second.

a_tiny_monthly_bill
# A small Python web app, running all month:
small server (1 CPU, 1 GB)  : 730 hrs × $0.010  = $ 7.30
storage for your files      : 10 GB  × $0.023  = $ 0.23
data sent to users          : 5 GB   × $0.090  = $ 0.45
                                            ─────────
                              total/month  ≈ $ 8.00

# Forgot to turn off a big GPU box? That same month: $1,000+ 😱
The famous beginner mistake: spinning up something powerful, forgetting it, and getting a shock bill. Always set a billing alert. The meter never sleeps.
>> The landscape

Who rents, and why location matters

AWS

Amazon. The biggest, oldest, most services.

Azure

Microsoft. Big in companies & offices.

Google Cloud

Google. Strong in data & AI.

Regions & availability zones. Each provider has data centers worldwide — Mumbai, Singapore, Frankfurt. You pick the one closest to your users so the ST2 journey is short and the app feels fast. Spread across zones, and a whole building can fail without taking you down.
Hosting in Mumbai vs Virginia can be the difference between a 30 ms and a 250 ms page load for an Indian user. Geography is still real, even in "the cloud".
>> Bringing it home

Your Python script, on the internet

Everything you've written runs on your laptop. With the cloud, the same file can run on a rented computer that's online 24/7 — reachable by anyone, anywhere.
