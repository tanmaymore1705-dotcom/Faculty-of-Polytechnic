DAY 21 / 40
>> 01 / 04 · The problem

You trust your bank. But should you have to?

When you send money to a friend, your bank updates a spreadsheet: subtract from your account, add to theirs. You never see that spreadsheet. You trust the bank completely.

The problem: What if the bank makes a mistake? Gets hacked? Goes bankrupt? Freezes your account? You have no control over your own money.
DAY 21 / 40
>> 02 / 04 · Ledgers

A ledger is just a list of who owns what

Traditional Ledger

A private spreadsheet inside a bank's server. Only the bank can read and write it. You see only your own transactions.

Blockchain Ledger

A public list of every transaction ever. Thousands of computers hold a copy. Anyone can verify any transaction.

Same idea, different control. Both record "Alice sent 10 to Bob." The difference is who gets to check — and who can change it.
DAY 21 / 40
>> 03 / 04 · Architecture

One copy vs many copies

CENTRALISED

One server holds the truth. Fast and easy to manage. Single point of failure — hack the server, game over.

DISTRIBUTED

Thousands of copies on thousands of machines. No single owner. To change the record you'd need to change ALL copies.

BLOCKCHAIN

A distributed ledger with a special rule: once a record is added, it is mathematically impossible to change it without detection.

Impossible to change? Yes — because every record is sealed by a cryptographic hash. We'll see exactly how in Day 22.
DAY 21 / 40
>> 04 / 04 · Key properties

What makes a blockchain special

IMMUTABLE

Once written, a record cannot be altered. Change one character and the entire chain breaks.

Example: You cannot rewrite a transaction from 2019.

DECENTRALISED

No single company or government owns it. Thousands of independent nodes all hold the same copy.

Example: Bitcoin runs on ~15,000 nodes worldwide.

TRANSPARENT

Every transaction is public. Anyone can open a block explorer and see every transfer ever made.

Note: Anonymity comes from addresses, not hidden data.

>> Connecting to Python

A ledger is just a Python list of dicts

ledger.py
ledger = [ {"from": "Alice", "to": "Bob", "amount": 10}, {"from": "Bob", "to": "Carol", "amount": 4}, {"from": "Alice", "to": "Carol", "amount": 6}, ] # Total balance for Bob: received = sum(tx["amount"] for tx in ledger if tx["to"] == "Bob") sent = sum(tx["amount"] for tx in ledger if tx["from"] == "Bob") print(f"Bob's balance: {received - sent}") # 6
FAMILIAR TOOLS Every Python concept here (lists, dicts, loops, f-strings) you already know. The blockchain is just a very clever list.
